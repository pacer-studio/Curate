Risk & Continuity

Questions every procurement team should ask

A platform choice is a 5-10 year commitment. These questions apply to every option on the table: continuing with SAP, adopting Shopify Plus, or commissioning a custom-built alternative.

What happens when key people leave?
SAP requires specialist SIs that are increasingly hard to retain. Custom platforms typically depend on the team that wrote the code. Shopify Plus has thousands of certified partners worldwide, so no single relationship is a point of failure.
Who handles PCI compliance after launch?
SAP Commerce includes PCI compliance, but that coverage ends when the contract expires. Shopify Plus is PCI DSS Level 1 certified automatically. Custom-built platforms that process payments typically need to self-certify annually: penetration testing, vulnerability scanning, and quarterly ASV scans.
Can it handle Black Friday at Reem Mall scale?
Shopify Plus processed $11.5B in BFCM 2024 globally with zero downtime. SAP requires pre-provisioned capacity planning. Custom platforms typically need manual scaling, load testing, and a dedicated war room for peak events.
How does each platform keep pace with innovation?
AI-powered shopping, agentic storefronts, and accelerated checkout are becoming table stakes. Shopify ships 150+ updates per year automatically. SAP's investment in Commerce Cloud is declining. Custom platforms ship only what the internal team builds, and every new feature competes with bug fixes.
Does Curate want to be in the platform business?
Maintaining SAP means retaining specialist SIs and managing complex upgrades. Running a custom platform means becoming a technology company: managing infrastructure, hiring engineers, and owning a product roadmap. Shopify Plus lets Curate focus on what it does best: luxury retail and marketplace curation.
What is the exit strategy?
SAP contract expiry is already forcing this conversation. With Shopify Plus, data is portable and the ecosystem provides standard migration paths. A bespoke platform typically locks the operator into a proprietary codebase with no standard migration tooling.
